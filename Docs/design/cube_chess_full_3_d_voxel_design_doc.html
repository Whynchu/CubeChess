01 · Overview

Project Overview

Cube Chess is a four-player strategy game played inside a transparent volumetric cube. This version is built as a true 3D system rather than a flat game wearing a 3D Halloween costume. Each piece occupies a full voxel cell, and all movement, control, obstruction, and capture logic operate within real three-axis space.

Voxel Occupation

Pieces live inside cells. They do not ride edges, vertices, or abstract beam intersections.

Full 3D First

Width, height, and depth all matter equally. The cube is the battlefield, not decorative framing.

Readability First

If players cannot tell where a piece is or where it can go, the design loses no matter how clever the math looks.

02 · Board

Board Specification

Grid Model

  • Board size: 8 × 8 × 8
  • Total cells: 512 voxels
  • Coordinate model: (x, y, z)
  • Every voxel is either empty or occupied by exactly one piece

Presentation Goals

  • Transparent outer cube shell
  • Faint visible voxel subdivision
  • Subtle atmospheric glow and depth fade
  • Optional slice emphasis for local readability

The board should feel like a space the player looks into, not a flat slab the player looks across. Empty space remains understated until needed, which keeps the board from turning into a chandelier full of arguments.

04 · Pieces

Piece Set, Occupation, and Collision

Per Player

  • 1 King
  • 1 Queen
  • 2 Rooks
  • 2 Bishops
  • 2 Knights

Occupation Rule

A piece occupies exactly one voxel. Allied pieces cannot enter it. Enemies may enter only through legal capture.

Collision Rule

Sliding movement stops at the first occupied voxel. Knights ignore intervening occupancy and only validate the destination.

Removing pawns keeps the ruleset cleaner and lets the spatial system breathe. In full 3D, even eight major pieces per player already create enough tactical density to keep the cube from feeling like an abandoned warehouse.

05 · Movement

Movement System

Movement is evaluated as legal voxel destinations, not as decorative path philosophy. Each piece family retains a recognizable chess identity while expanding into volumetric space.

Piece Movement Logic Direction Count Notes
Rook Slides along cardinal axes: +X / -X / +Y / -Y / +Z / -Z 6 Travels through empty voxels until blocked
Bishop Slides along plane diagonals in XY, XZ, and YZ planes 12 Each plane contributes four diagonal directions
Queen Combines rook, bishop, and true 3D diagonal movement 26 Most spatially dominant piece on the board
Knight 2 along one axis, 1 along another, 0 along the third Multiple jump targets Ignores intervening blockers
King Moves exactly one voxel in any adjacent legal direction 26 adjacent cells Local omnidirectional unit
06 · Formation

Starting Formation

Each faction begins in a corner tetrahedral cluster extending inward through three layers.

Layer 0 — Corner Tip

King

Layer 1 — Three Adjacent Inward Voxels

Queen · Bishop · Bishop

Layer 2 — Outer Inward Ring

Rook · Rook · Knight · Knight

This arrangement gives the king a strongly nested identity while preserving a clean geometric opening state. It also makes the whole board look like four compressed attack crystals aimed at the middle, which is a lot cooler than “everyone politely lines up and waits.”

07 · Camera

Full 3D Camera System

Core Requirements

  • Horizontal cube rotation through direct drag
  • Controlled vertical pitch adjustment
  • Minimal inertia and no floaty over-spin
  • Fast reorientation after rotation

Readability Support

  • Optional camera easing toward selected piece
  • Transparency reduction on obstructing geometry
  • Slice or local-region emphasis during piece selection
  • Potential angle snap assists at 45° or 90° intervals

The camera is a gameplay tool, not a cinematic toy. If the player loses the active piece every time they rotate, the system is doing slapstick instead of strategy.

08 · Visuals

Board, Piece, and Move Visualization

Board Readability

  • Faint wireframe subdivision
  • Soft outer shell glow
  • Depth haze instead of visual clutter

Occupied Voxels

  • Bright faction-colored cell base
  • Visible occupancy emphasis
  • Clear distinction from empty space

Move Display

  • Legal destinations shown as voxel markers
  • Capture cells shown more aggressively
  • Sliding path previews kept secondary and subtle

Capture Feedback

On capture, the attacking piece resolves into the destination voxel, the target voxel flashes, the enemy piece collapses or dissolves, and the original origin cell becomes empty. The effect should be sharp and quick, ideally in the 100–180 ms range.

09 · Interaction

Interaction Flow

Turn Interaction Loop

  1. Inspect or rotate the cube
  2. Select a friendly occupied voxel
  3. Reveal legal destination voxels
  4. Select a destination
  5. Resolve move and capture feedback
  6. Pass turn to the next player

Support Systems

  • Last move origin and destination highlight
  • Threat indicators when relevant
  • Optional active-depth emphasis
  • Reduced opacity on obstructing geometry
10 · Rules

Rule Identity and Turn Structure

Turn Order

Recommended baseline rotation:

  • Yellow
  • Red
  • Purple
  • Blue

Elimination Condition

Recommended baseline: king capture rather than checkmate.

  • Cleaner for multiplayer
  • Better for early AI prototypes
  • Less rules overhead in dense 3D states

This system is no longer standard chess stretched awkwardly into a cube. It becomes a volumetric territorial combat game with recognizable chess ancestry and much nastier center pressure.

11 · Prototype

Prototype Requirement

The first prototype should validate readability before it validates strategy depth.

Prototype Feature Scope

  • Full 8×8×8 voxel board
  • Real 3D camera rotation
  • Occupied voxel display
  • Selection and movement on touch
  • Collision handling and capture support
  • Per-piece destination visualization

Validation Criteria

  • Selected voxel identified instantly
  • Legal moves understood within ~2 seconds
  • Blocked vs open routes distinguished cleanly
  • Rotation does not destroy orientation

Recommended Test Order

Rook → Bishop → Queen → Knight → King

If rook or bishop readability fails, the rest of the system is already on fire. No need to invite the queen in to help burn the house down.

12 · Final Identity

Final Identity Statement

Cube Chess is a 4-player voxel-based 3D strategy game in which chess-derived pieces occupy and contest real cubic space inside a transparent battlefield. Its identity depends on full volumetric movement, strong corner-based spatial conflict, camera-supported readability, and clean tactical clarity inside dense 3D space.

The target feeling is not “normal chess but stranger.” The target feeling is a clean futuristic territorial war unfolding inside a living cube.
